פתוחה:  
A Parsha that ends in the middle of a line, and 
the following Parsha begins on the next line. 
(There are 290 such Parshios in Torah.)  
סתומה 
A Parsha that ends in the middle of a line, and 
the following Parsha begins on that same line 
(after a space of about 9 letters). 
(There are 379 such Parshyos in the Torah.)  
A פרשה פתוחה signifies a new 
topic, and a פרשה סתומה 
signifies a connection to the 
previous topic.

Content Appendix – Standard 2 
Introduction 
Standard 2 of the Zekelman Standards for חומשdeals with חומשcontent. In creating the חומשstandards, 
we have decided not to provide a scope and sequence for חומשcontent, but to leave the specifics of this 
standard largely of to individual schools, curriculum specialists, and teachers. There are several reasons 
for this.  
The obvious reasons are is that listing each and every name, place, theme and so forth that appears in 
the חומשwould simply not be feasible in the format that we used for standards, and also would 
constitute too much information for students to be expected to master. Additionally, however, there is a 
certain flexibility to which particular content a school might choose to assign to each grade.   
Schools may take various factors into consideration when establishing their syllabus for content. Some 
of these factors include the time available for teaching חומשi.e., scheduling concerns; the need to align 
a syllabus with other schools in a particular community, to ease transition between them; or other 
matters of preference. All of the remaining חומשstandards can be taught via basically any syllabus 
covering a reasonable sampling of the פרשיותin חומשso the issue of which particular פרשיות ,פרקיםand 
פסוקיםto cover is not of major concern. 
However, to support Standard 2, we are providing a suggested list of content items as an Appendix. The 
intention of this appendix is to serve as a resource for teachers, which they can use to map out the 
content that they intend to cover, and to aid them in selecting specific content items to teach. A few 
things to keep in mind as you use this Appendix: 
 The Appendix covers ויחי –פרשיות לך לךwhich is frequently the syllabus for חומשin lower 
elementary grades. The Appendix is divided by פרשהand is further divided into two tables per 
פרשהConcepts and Information – for convenience and ease of use. 
 
 The categories in each document are the same as the categories for content specified in 
Standard 2. The only difference is that there is no column for “מצוותin the Appendix, since 
much fewer מצוותappear in ספר בראשיתas in subsequent ספריםof חומשInstead, any Mitzvos 
that appear in the text are included in “Concepts- מושגים ”.
 
 There is no need to teach every item in the Appendix. On the contrary, many of the details 
should only be taught in an older grade. Use the Appendix as a guide, selecting items to teach 
and to focus on, according to your curriculum, syllabus, and your students’ abilities (as well as 
the suggested scope and sequence included in this appendix – see below – if you choose).

HIGH-FREQUENCY VOCABULARY  
 
Some years ago, Rabbi Manfred Pick undertook a research study regarding 
the language structure of בראשית. It was the purpose of his study to develop 
a vocabulary list which would include all the words found in בראשית with 
their respective frequencies and to make a statistical analysis of the 
frequency distribution of the text of בראשית. 
 
The study revealed that the Book of בראשית consists of a total number of 
16,258 words (exclusive of pronouns) which may be reduced to a total o f 
1,545 “basic word forms. ” These 1 ,545 words occur with varying 
frequencies and the range of their frequency distribution extends from a 
frequency of one to a frequency of over 600. The majority of the 1,545 
words occur a very limited number of times with 621 words or 40.2% of the 
total appearing only once (for example: אד. מחרand 1,077 words or 69.7% 
of the total appearing less than five times (for example: מועד. מאןHowever, 
there are a lso a number of words which occur with a relatively high 
frequency: 146 of the 1,545 words or 9.4% of the total appear 20 times and 
over (for example: הר. חמשה67 of the words or 4.4% of the total appear 
51 times and over (for example: ידע. כי ;) and one word (the word אמר )
appears a total of 601 times.  
 
The study, furthermore, disclosed that a relatively small number of high - 
frequency words are responsible for a large portion of the total text of 
בראשיתIt was found that 70.7% of the total text is made up of the 146 
words that appear 20 times and over; 80.8% of the total text is made up of 
the 270 words that appear 10 times and ove r; and 88.9% of the total text is 
made up of the 468 words that appear 5 times and over.

The above findings have a profound bearing upon the teaching of the חומש 
in the lower grades. The fact that 40% of the words of בראשית occur only 
once indicates tha t great emphasis ought to be placed upon the utilization 
of contextual clues. It goes without saying that not only every word but 
every letter found in the תורה has particular significance. It follows that the 
pupils’ comprehension of the חומש of necessity presupposes his thorough 
understanding of every detail of the text. Yet, there need not be a 
compulsion on the part of the teacher to have the weaker students retain 
the meaning of the more difficult and less frequently used words as “words 
by themselves.” On the contrary, the teacher should be fully satisfied if the 
weaker student will understand these words in the חומש verse on the basis 
of context and general content.  
 
Of e qually great importance is the f act that a relatively small number o f 
words are constantly repeated  and, therefore, account for a l arge share of 
the text of בראשית. It would seem that these high -frequency words ought to 
be thoroughly mastered by all of the students. It is strongly recommended 
that special attention be given to the 270 high  frequency words. Once a 
high-frequency word is encountered in a פסוק, it should be drilled to the 
point of m astery by the students. Thereafter, the student should be held 
responsible for the meaning of the word when encountering this word in a 
new פסוק. .In addition, periodically all of the high -frequency words 
encountered should be reviewed and drilled so that the mastery of their 
meaning be assured and retained.

Plural noun endings 
1. Plural masculine nouns usually end in *ֶ ם  .

sometimes called the ‘reversing vav’ in English is actually referred to 
by grammarians as the ‘vav consecutive’. 
These two names indicate the role of this vav. This vav is commonly found at the 
It indicates that the verb should be understood in the 
opposite tense from the way it is written. Thus if the vav precedes a verb in the 
future tense (imperfect) it actually means that it should be understood in past 
(perfect) tense. If the vav precedes a verb in the past tense (perfect tense) it should 
be understood as occurring in the future (imperfect) tense. Hence the name 
‘reversing vav’ or ‘vav conversive’ (because of its converting effect). 
The term ‘vav consecutive’ is derived from the fact that this changing of tense 
usually occurs when the verb is part of a continuous narrative. When the first verb 
in the narrative is in the perfect or imperfect tense in their regular forms (without a 
vav preceding them), then the following verbs with a vav in front of them, 
although written in the opposite tense, are understood in the same tense. The vav 
indicates that they are consecutive to the initial verb.

Appendix to 3.6 G 
The Word את 
By Mrs. Sara Rosenfeld 
 
The word את is used in two different ways in the Torah. Most workbooks will say, one is 
untranslatable and the other is ‘with’.  
However, there is actually a more precise explanation.  
The untranslatable word את is actually a marker for the direct object when it is also a 
definite noun. 
 
What is a direct object? Most sentences have three basic parts, a subject, a verb and a 
direct object. 
The subject is the one doing the action. 
The verb is the action. 
The direct object is the person or thing receiving the action. 
For example: Sholom threw a ball.  
Sholom is the subject. 
Threw is the verb. 
Ball is the direct object. 
 
What is a definite noun? 
In English, there are definite and indefinite nouns. Indefinite means that it is not specific, 
and in English would be signified by the word ‘a’.  For example, ‘a ball’, or ‘a house’ are 
indefinite nouns. 
A definite noun is specific, and in English we would say ‘the ball’, or ‘the house’. 
In לשון קודש there is no word to signify an indefinite noun, like the word “a” in English. A 
noun itself without any prefixes is by definition indefinite. I.e., ילד is ‘a boy.’ 
To make something definite, the letter ה can be added, i.e. הילד  - the boy’. Or, by making a 
noun possessive, we also turn it into a definite noun without having to add anything else. 
I.e., בִּתּו  is his daughter, and no one else’s daughter, so it is a definite noun. 
Proper nouns - names of people/places – are automatically definite nouns.

Thus, a definite noun in לשון קודש can have any of the following characteristics: 
 It has the ה' הידיעה added as a prefix. (הילד, הבית) 
 It is a possessive noun. (אחיו, בתי) 
 It is a proper noun. (דוד, שרה, כנען) 
 
Summary 
Thus, את has 2 definitions: 
1) The word with, e.g. "את הא-לקים התהלך נח"  
2) The untranslatable definition, which actually is a marker for a definite noun acting as 
a direct object. Think of it as an arrow pointing an action to its specific recipient, e.g. 
"ויולד נחאת שם את חם ואת יפת"
