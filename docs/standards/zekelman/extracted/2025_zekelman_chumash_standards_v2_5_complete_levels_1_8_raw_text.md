I. About the Zekelman Standards  for Chumash  
 
How do we gauge the success of our teaching? More importantly, how do we know if we are doing our job as educators, and if we are 
doing it effectively? 
It is clear that it would be futile to embark on a road trip, map and provisions in hand, without any idea of where you are g oing. 
Similarly, the only way for teaching to be considered successful is when the teacher has clear goals in mind. As clearly arti culated 
benchmarks for mastery in each subject, learning standards tell us where we are headed in the journey of education, and give us a 
means to determine our success. 
The Menachem Education Foundation, with the self-stated mission of professionalizing education in Jewish schools, has taken up the 
cause of learning standards and facilitated an unprecedented collaborative effort among educators across the globe to develop them. 
Many of the standards incorporate cognitive skills and various levels of Bloom’s Taxonomy; and we have customized the framework 
and content of learning standards for use in the unique arena of Judaic subjects. 
At the Menachem Education Foundation, we believe that all Jewish students should have the opportunity to master the academic 
skills necessary for self -sufficiency in Torah study, confident engagement with Torah text, and joyous pursuit of lifelong Jewish 
learning and living in all areas. The Zekelman Standards for Chumash were created to make these goals a reality, for EVERY child, 
in EVERY school. 
These standards form a living document. They were created through extensive deliberation, the input of a variety of education al 
professionals, and real -time feedback from teachers who have used them. Over the past years, these standards have been 
continuously examined, revised, and updated, as new minds joined its team of contributors and new data from schools and classrooms 
emerged. In 2014, the standards were completed through grade 8 for the first time, and in 2017, a new layer of review and revision 
yielded the Zekelman Standards for Chumash 2.0. Now in 2025, Version 2.5 includes further updates that have been made as more 
data is gathered from schools implementing these standards. 
By using these standards, you are becoming a member in the process of establishing a standard for the learning that takes pla ce in 
our schools. We hope to hear from you, and wish you much success in your journey of teaching.

III. Introduction  to the Zekelman  
Standards  for Chumash  2.0 
 
The Zekelman Standards for Chumash present a significant 
milestone for Torah educators, but not necessarily an innovation. A 
closer look at our sources shows that an approach to Chumash that 
is based on skills and mastery, as opposed to coverage alone, is 
deeply rooted in in our heritage.  
 
The Maharal, commenting1 on the word “Veshinantam” which 
connotes fluency and ownership, notes the unfortunate prevalence 
in his day of a rushed approach to Limud HaTorah. He refers to the 
Mishna in Avos2 that delineates a sequential approach to Torah 
study: ben chamesh limikra, ben eser limishna, and so forth. He 
bemoans the fact that that students are rushed to cover ground and 
go from one level to the next without taking the time to ensure that 
they have acquired the proper foundation for the next stage in 
learning. After all that, they leave Yeshiva not having “acquired” the 
Torah - in the sense of a “kinyan,” connoting fluency and 
comprehension. The Maharal underscores the importance of a 
sequence that makes mastery of each step a prerequisite to the 
next, and this is the conceptual foundation of learning standards. 
 
A few centuries later, the Alter Rebbe made a ruling along similar 
lines in Hilchos Talmud Torah. In today’s day and age, wrote the 
Alter Rebbe, it is more important for students to learn some 
Gemara deeply, than a lot of Gemara without the accompanying 
analysis. The reason for this is that educators need to equip 
students with the ability to learn in depth on their own, even after 
leaving Yeshiva.

IV. Portrait of a Student
The Zekelman Standards for Chumash were created with the 
following vision for eighth grade Yeshiva school graduates in mind. 
1. They can independently learn  – decode, translate, and 
comprehend – significant portions of חומש text. They can 
identify new information given, conceptualize textual 
narratives, and integrate the “פשטmeaning that the חומש 
conveys. They can comfortably orient themselves in  the text 
of the חומשin order to conduct research, find desired 
locations, and easily navigate between different places in the 
חומש. 
2. They engage intellectually  with the חומש text. They can 
identify deviations in the text and its conventions, and 
articulate appropriate questions about such deviations. They 
can analyze the text, in order to glean nuances which allow 
them to reconcile puzzling aspects of the plain text, and in 
order to construe a deeper and subtler understanding of what 
the חומש is saying. 
3. They are comfortable with מפרשים as guides  and 
companions in their learning journey. They have the skills to 
locate, read and understand a number of מפרשים – including, 
most fundamentally,  רש"י – and can integrate the 
commentary’s insights and additions into their 
comprehension of the text. They can identify when to turn to 
a particular מפורש for a particular purpose, and they use the 
thought processes of the מפרשים for further analysis and 
ever deeper comprehension of and engagement with the text. 
4. They possess a comprehensive and involved familiarity with a 
significant portion of חומש texts, topics and narratives. The 
knowledge gleaned from their eight years of learning חומש 
will form the scaffolding for their greater Judaic knowledge 
and further learning.  
5. They approach the study of חומש and other תורה texts with 
value for their  role, relevance, and holiness,  integrated 
throughout their eight years of learning about the חומשboth 
from the teacher and from the חומש itself, and interacting 
with the חומש in positive ways. 
6. They possess moral, spiritual, and intellectual 
understandings, gleaned from deep and meaningful study of a 
variety of selections within the text of the חומשThese 
understandings, now integrated into the students’ 
personality, character and intelligence, are a prism through 
which to approach continued learning, and life situations. 
7. Their fluency in, comfort with, and integration of all of the 
components of חומש study mastered over eight years of 
learning enables them to: 
a. Have the confidence that will contribute to 
motivation to tackle additional texts of increasing 
challenge, both in חומש and other areas of תורה. 
b. Integrate study of חומש and, by extension, other 
areas of תורה )into their own person, as a cherished, 
valued and enjoyable activity to be proactively and 
independently pursued.

V. Using the Standards  
The Zekelman Standards for Chumash are designed in a similar 
fashion to the Common Core Standards developed by 
educators for teaching secular studies. Many of our standards 
overlap with the Common Core Standards, incorporating 
essential cognitive and learning skills, and all of the levels of 
Bloom’s taxonomy. 
We have added an additional layer to our standards in order to 
pre-empt the problem of what educators call “unpacking the 
standards.” In order to help teachers understand the standards 
and make them usable in the classroom, we incorporated 
Enduring Understa ndings, Essential Questions and Key 
Knowledge (three components of the curriculum model  
Understanding by Design). 
With this background in mind, you can now approach the actual 
standards, which, with some variation, are organized in the 
following way: 
1. Enduring Understandings (EUs)   These are full sentences 
which are called Enduring Understandings because they 
present principles that endure beyond the classroom and 
often beyond the actual subject being studied.  They are 
overarching conceptual understandings which underpin 
large parts of the student’s learning. The EUs are not 
actually verbalized by the teacher in class. Rather, they are 
discovered and understood by the students as a result of 
the content explored, discussed or reached as a result of the 
specific content that you convey. Keep EUs in mind 
throughout your teaching, with the goal of conveying them 
through the means of your curriculum. 
2. Essential Questions (EQs)  A question becomes an essential 
question when it meets the following criteria: 
a. It gets to the heart of the material under discussion  
or is needed for learning core content. 
b. It is thought provoking and stimulates rethinking of 
big ideas, assumptions and prior lessons. 
c. It has no ‘simple’ right answer but is meant to be 
argued and defended with numerous examples. 
d. It can be asked repeatedly, within the same year and 
over the years. 
e. It often leads to further questions. 
Essential questions are posed by the teacher in a recurring 
fashion. Select EQs, dependent on the content and skill 
being studied in any one lesson, should be visible on the 
classroom walls or on the board. It is expected that students 
will eventually ask themselves these questions as they 
study the text.  Our lists of EQs corresponding to each EU 
(see above) are not exhaustive, and we often provided 
several examples for each. Feel free to think of more  that 
meet the above criteria.

3. Key Knowledge  The key knowledge preceding each 
standard area lays out the fundamentals underlying all of 
the sub-standards in that area. In many cases, these include 
grammar rules, or essential facts, which are beyond the 
scope of particular  standards but are necessary for the 
students to know in order to be able to perform the skill 
outlined in the actual standards.  Teachers should become 
thoroughly familiar with the key knowledge, even if some 
of it surpasses the scope of the skills which that teacher’s 
grade must master. This will ensur e that the teacher 
possesses a complete understanding of the subject matter 
at h and, and can then choose which aspects of the key 
knowledge to explicitly teach to their class.  
4. Scope and Sequence   This section is the main part of the 
standards document. After the understandings, questions 
and knowledge introducing each area of the standards, you 
will find a table which lays out what is considered to be 
mastery of that particular area of learning, for each grade. 
If a particular item is extended beyond one grade, continue 
to assess it to ensure retention for as long as it extends. 
5. Hebrew Terms and Expressions  Throughout the standards, 
we have used Hebrew terms, expressions, and מאמרי חז"ל .
A teacher should use this vocabulary while teaching  the 
corresponding standards, and assess the students for 
mastery of this vocabulary in addition to its concurrent 
skills. A list of all assessable terms and expressions can be 
found in the Appendix. 
6. Appendices Understanding and using these standards 
requires the help of some background information, further 
elaboration, and reference pages. These are included in the 
extensive appendices to this document.  
 
Using the primary components of Data Driven Instruction – 
assessment, analysis, and action – will help you ensure that 
your students continue to progress towards mastery of these 
standards. Assessments have an additional benefit for the 
teacher – they clarify and refine our expectations of a student. 
Whenever you are unsure about how to teach a particular 
standard, think about how you will assess it. Additional 
resources provided by the Menachem Education Foundation 
can assist you in implementing both the what – the Chumash 
Standards – and the how – Data Driven Instruction – that 
comprise standards based learning at its best.

VII. What Standards a re Not  
As much as it is important to understand what the standards are, it is 
important to understand what they are not. For example, standards 
do not lock teachers into particular curriculum content or teaching 
methods, taking away their creative autonomy. Standards also do not 
do the teaching in the teacher’s stead, taking away the educator’s 
responsibility. The following are particular areas which will require 
your separate attention while using these standards. 
1. Syllabus These standards are not specific to any particular 
syllabus. They can be taught using any parsha or perek of 
Chumash. However, since many schools teach the Chumashim of 
Bereishis and Shemos in grades 1 -8, some details of the 
standards are based upon the assumption that that is the syllabus 
you will most likely be using.  
On the other hand, bear in mind that these standards value 
quality over quantity. Using them properly might slow down your 
teaching in the sense that less material will be covered. You may 
want to rethink your syllabus in this light, but the reward will 
certainly be worthwhile. 
2. Curriculum Standards are not the same thing as a curriculum. 
They do not tell you what to teach, nor how to teach it,  rather 
they indicate the benchmarks that students should have attained 
at the end of each year of their schooling. It is up to the teachers 
and the curriculum coordinators of each school to determine the 
curriculum and the teaching methods which will ensu re that 
these standards are met, and it is up to each teacher to take 
ownership of the components of Chumash mastery in order to 
ensure that it takes place for each student.  
As such, it would be self-defeating to provide teachers with end-
product curricula without empowering teachers to engage with 
the standards themselves. However, we do wish to support 
teachers in this process, and are building a growing database of 
curricular resources and suggested teaching strategies for 
teachers to work with.   
3. Assessments Standards are the destination for the journey of 
learning Chumash, but it is up to the teacher to make sure that 
every student keeps up on that road. To this end, it is crucial for 
a teacher to create and administer assessments to gauge each 
student’s progress. As mentioned earlier, standards are designed 
to be assessable and to work in conjunction with assessments.  
 
Standard based assessments form the first step of a three part 
process called Data Driven Instruction (DDI). The second and third  
steps are to analyze assessment results, and c reate action plans 
based on the data gleaned, in order to ensure that each student will 
attain mastery. The Menachem Education Foundation promotes the 
implementation of DDI as the best way to ensure the success of 
standard based learning.  Additional resources to support standards

2. Content 
A major part of the goal of learning חומשin addition to fulfilling the obligation of לימוד תורה, is to learn its content:  the history, life lessons, 
personalities, and instructions which are our national heritage. Although schools may differ in their particular syllabus choices as to which 
פרשיות or פרקים to cover, students in grades 1 through 6 can amass a large and valuable base of Torah knowledge. The essence of the Mitzvah 
of learning Torah is mastery of its content – הs communication to the Jewish people. 
 
Note to teachers: This standard provides an opportunity for students who struggle with other areas of learning חומש to shine. While engaging 
successfully with the text and language of חומש can prove challenging for many students, those same students may excel in engaging with the 
content learned from the חומשindependent of the text it is derived from. 
Also note that the standards do not dictate a school’s choice of syllabus and which content is covered. For suggested content items in ספר
בראשיתsee the Appendix to Standard 2.

Key Knowledge: (for 3.05 Pronouns) 
1. A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun. 
2. Nouns can serve as either a subject or object:  who or what is doing the action is the subject, and who or what is receiving the action 
is the object. 
3. Different kinds of pronouns replace nouns, depending on whether they are the subject or object. (For example, אני refers to a 
subject and is a subjective pronoun, and אותי refers to an object and is an objective pronoun, just like in English, “I” refers to a 
subject, and “me” refers to an object.) 
4. Pronouns also appear in other forms depending on how they are used in the sentence. For example, there are demonstrative 
pronouns to point something out (e.g. this, those - זה, זאת ,)...interrogative pronouns to ask questions (e.g. who, what, etc. - מי
מה ,)and prepositional pronouns to describe relationships in time and place (e.g. with, to, etc. - אלי, אתי, עמי, ממני.)  
5. For every pronoun, we need to figure out who or what it is talking about. This is the pronoun’s referent (what the pronoun refers to). 
Pronouns match their referent nouns in gender and number. 
6. There is no such thing as “it” in לה"קSince all nouns, not only people, are either זכר or נקבה ,the pronouns that replace them are 
always זכר or נקבה as well. 
7. In לה"קpronouns are often indicated by prefixes and suffixes attached to other words, instead of being written separately. 
Note to Teachers:  The first standard for each kind of pronoun is the ability to use that kind of pronoun in English. This standard should be taught 
as a fundamental, introductory unit to pronouns, in which the teacher points out to students how pronouns are used and their different varieties, 
and students have the opportunity to use them in a class discussion or exercise. The English pronouns themselves may not be new to first graders, 
but this הקדמה will make them more aware of the kinds of pronouns we use and the roles that they play.

Key Knowledge: (for 3.06 Prepositions, Conjunctions and the Definite Article) 
1. A preposition is a word that indicates the relationship between two things in time and place (or other relationships), e.g. on, with,

2. A conjunction combines two words – i.e. and.

5. A פסוק may contain one or more ‘English’ sentences. 
6. The simplest Hebrew sentence comprises of a noun and a verb or an adjective. 
7. The person doing the action or the person or item being described in a sentence is called the subject. “Who” is doing the action or “Who 
or what” is this talking about?  
8. The person or thing that receives the action (verb) is the direct object of the sentence.
