There are two big differences between an adjective in Hebrew and one in English. 
In English the adjective is said or written before the noun it describes.  I n לשון קודש 
it is nearly always the other way around. 
For example when you say, a big boy, the adjective big is said or written before the 
noun (boy) that it describes.  The same sentence in לשון קודש would be ילד גדולThe 
noun ילד is first and then the a djective גדול comes second. That is one of the 
reasons why it does not always make sense when you translate a  פסוקfrom לשון
קודש directly to English in the same word order. 
The other big difference is that in English the adjective stays the same regardles s of 
the noun it describes.  ‘Big’ will stay the same in each of these descriptions; the big 
boy, the big girl, the big boys, the big girls. In לשון קודש  the adjective changes to 
match the noun depending on whether it is זכר or נקבה and יחיד or רביםThe 
adjective usually has the same ending (suffix) as the noun it is describing.

The סמיכות Form 
In English one way of saying that one noun belongs to another noun is by using the 
words “a” or “the” and “of”.

would mean by this that a book or the specific book belongs to the boy.

טעמי המקרא  – Trop” 
 
A ספר תורה  has no punctuation. Part of our מסורה and what משה רבינו  taught בני 
ישראלwas how to read the תורה correctly. This included how to pronounce the 
words and how to ‘sing’ the words (as in when a בעל קורא  reads the תורה in shul).  
At first this was passed down orally from generation to generati on. Now, in our 
printed חומשיםwe see this מסורה through two main items – the נקודות and the טעמי 
המקרא. 
The טעמי המקרא  serve three important functions: 
1. They tell us how to sing the words and the פסוקים. 
2. They tell us which part of the word needs to be emphasized. The טעמים are written on 
the accented syllable of the word. 
3. They tell us where to stop, or pause and take a breath (related to number 1) which 
indicates how to break up the פסוק.

Prepositions 
A preposition is a part of speech, just like a noun or a verb. It connects a noun or 
pronoun to another word in the sentence, showing us the relationship between 
them. Prepositions usually answer the questions where? or when?, telling us about 
a person or object’s location in either time or space. There are over 150 
prepositions. The following are some of them: after, as, at, before, following, for, 
from, in, like, near, on, over, past, since, to.

Subjects and Objects 
Most English sentences have a subject, a verb and  an object. The subject is the 
person or thing doing the action (the verb) in the sentence. The object is the person 
or thing receiving the action. For example: “Chaim threw the book”. Chaim is doing 
the action. Chaim is the subject. The book is what he threw, the book is the object. 
Most sentences in לשון קודש also have a subject, verb and an object. Most pronouns 
in English (aside from ‘it’ and ‘you’) and all pronouns in לשון קודש  will change 
depending on whether the noun they are replacing is the subject  or the object of a 
sentence.
