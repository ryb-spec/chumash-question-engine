## Examples of weak distractor risk

- A distractor can be too obviously different in topic, length, or story moment.
- A distractor can let students answer from narrative memory instead of reading the Hebrew phrase.
- A distractor can mix single-word vocabulary with a phrase-level target in a way that makes the question feel uneven.
- A distractor can be valid elsewhere in Perek 3 but still too easy to eliminate for the target phrase.

## Examples of acceptable distractor direction

- Distractors should generally be phrase-level, not isolated single words.
- Distractors should be wrong for the selected phrase but plausible enough to require attention to the Hebrew.
- Distractors should not be synonyms or alternate acceptable translations.
- Distractors should avoid theological/context claims beyond the reviewed source row.
- Distractors should preserve source/provenance and remain teacher-reviewable.
